Ram Janmabhoomi Temple
Historical Background
  • Mythological and Ancient Background:
    Ayodhya in Hindu Tradition: Ayodhya is revered as the birthplace of Lord Rama, one of the most worshipped deities in Hinduism. According to the ancient epic, the Ramayana, Ayodhya was the capital of the Kosala kingdom, ruled by King Dasharatha, the father of Lord Rama.

  • Medieval Period:
    Babri Masjid Construction: In 1528, during the reign of the Mughal emperor Babur, a mosque known as the Babri Masjid was constructed at the site.
Architectural Features
  • Design and Layout
    The temple follows the traditional Nagara style, characterized by a shikhara (spire) over the sanctum sanctorum (garbhagriha). The design is inspired by classical Hindu temple architecture seen in historic temples across North India.

  • Sanctum Sanctorum (Garbhagriha)
    The inner sanctum will house the idol of Lord Rama, along with idols of Sita, Lakshman, Bharat, Shatrughna, and Hanuman. The sanctum is the most sacred part of the temple, and its architecture is designed to focus all attention on the deity.
Religious Significance
  • Birthplace of Lord Rama
    The site of the Ram Mandir is believed to be the exact birthplace of Lord Rama, an avatar of the god Vishnu and a central figure in Hinduism. According to Hindu scriptures, Ayodhya is the birthplace of Rama, who is revered as the ideal king, son, husband, and leader.

  • Connection to the Ramayana
    The Ramayana, one of Hinduism's most important epics, recounts the life, struggles, and triumphs of Lord Rama. Ayodhya, as his birthplace, is intrinsically connected to the epic's narrative.
Food Practices
  • Prasadam
    In Hindu temples, food offerings made to the deity are called prasadam. At the Ram Mandir, prasadam is prepared with great devotion and care, often using ingredients that are considered pure and sattvic.

  • Ritualistic Offering
    Before being distributed to devotees, the prepared food is first offered to the deity, in this case, Lord Rama, in a ritual known as bhoga.
Banke Bihari Temple, VRINDAVAN
Festivals
Janmashtami
Janmashtami celebrates the birth of Lord Krishna, who is believed to have been born at midnight on the eighth day (ashtami) of the dark fortnight (Krishna Paksha) in the Hindu month of Bhadrapada (August-September).
Holi
Holi, the festival of colors, marks the arrival of spring and celebrates the divine love of Radha and Krishna. It is one of the most vibrant and widely celebrated festivals in Vrindavan.

Radhashtami
Radhashtami celebrates the birth of Radha, Krishna's beloved consort and the personification of his divine love. It falls on the eighth day of the bright fortnight (Shukla Paksha) in the Hindu month of Bhadrapada.
Religious significance

Land of Krishna’s Pastimes: Vrindavan is believed to be the place where Lord Krishna spent his childhood and youth. The town is the setting for many of the most cherished stories of Krishna.
Center of Bhakti: Vrindavan is a pivotal center for the Bhakti movement, which emphasizes devotion to God as the path to spiritual liberation. Many saints, poets, and spiritual leaders like Chaitanya Mahaprabhu, Vallabhacharya, and Swami Haridas.

Architectural Features

Shikhara: Many temples in Vrindavan follow the Nagara style, characterized by a prominent shikhara or spire that rises over the sanctum sanctorum (garbhagriha). This spire is often intricately decorated with carvings and sculptures.
Mandapa: The temples usually feature a mandapa or pillared hall in front of the sanctum, where devotees gather for prayers and rituals. The mandapas are often open on all sides, allowing light and air to flow through.

Food Practices

Vegetarianism: Vrindavan is a strictly vegetarian town, reflecting the belief that all life is sacred. The diet excludes not only meat but also eggs, onion, and garlic, which are considered tamasic in many Hindu traditions.
Festive Foods: On Krishna’s birthday, Janmashtami, special dishes are prepared and offered to him, including makhan-mishri (fresh butter with sugar crystals), panjiri (a sweet made from roasted flour, sugar, and ghee), and kheer (rice pudding).

kashi vishwanath temple Varanasi
History:
The Kashi Vishwanath Temple in Varanasi is one of the most revered and ancient Hindu temples dedicated to Lord Shiva. Located on the western bank of the holy river Ganga, it is a major pilgrimage site for Hindus and is considered one of the twelve Jyotirlingas, the holiest of Shiva temples.
The Kashi Vishwanath Temple is said to be the site where the original Jyotirlinga, a fiery column of light representing Shiva, manifested, making it a central place of worship.
Architecture:
The Kashi Vishwanath Temple in Varanasi is an architectural marvel that blends religious significance with historical and cultural elements. The temple's architecture is a reflection of its long and varied history, having been destroyed and rebuilt several times, each time incorporating different architectural styles.br> The Kashi Vishwanath Temple is relatively compact, reflecting the dense urban fabric of Varanasi. The temple complex is built around a central shrine, the sanctum sanctorum (garbhagriha).
Festivals:
Maha Shivaratri: Maha Shivaratri is the most important festival celebrated at the Kashi Vishwanath temple.
Shravan Maas: The month of Shravan (July-August) is considered highly auspicious for the worship of Lord Shiva. It is believed that during this month, Shiva’s energy is most potent.
Kartik Purnima: Kartik Purnima, which falls on the full moon day in the Hindu month of Kartik, is another major festival at the Kashi Vishwanath Temple.
