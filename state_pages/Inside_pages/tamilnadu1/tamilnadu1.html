LET'S EXPLORE THE
TEMPLES OF TAMILNADU

Ekambaranathar Temple
Historical Background
  • Ancient Origin:
    The temple gained prominence during the Pallava dynasty, which ruled the region from the 6th to 9th centuries. The Pallavas were great patrons of art and architecture, and they significantly contributed to the temple’s development.

  • Development under Various Dynasties:
    The temple complex was inaugurated on November 6, 2005, by the then-President of India, Dr. A.P.J. Abdul Kalam.
Architectural Features
  • Dravidian Architecture
    The temple is built in the Dravidian architectural style, which is characterized by large gopurams (gateway towers), pillared halls, and expansive courtyards. This style is prominent in South Indian temples and is known for its intricate stone carvings and towering structures.

  • Rajagopuram
    The Rajagopuram of the Ekambaranathar Temple is one of its most striking features. Standing at 59 meters (194 feet), it is one of the tallest temple towers in South India. Constructed in 1509 CE by the Vijayanagara king Krishnadevaraya.
Religious Significance
  • Association with the Element of Earth
    Pancha Bhoota Sthalam: The Ekambaranathar Temple is one of the Pancha Bhoota Sthalams, which are five temples dedicated to Lord Shiva, each representing one of the five elements of nature—earth, water, fire, air, and space.

  • Mythological Significance
    The temple's mythology is closely tied to a story where Goddess Parvati, seeking to reunite with Shiva after a disagreement, performed rigorous penance at the site where the temple now stands. She fashioned a Lingam from sand to worship Shiva.
Food Practices
  • Prasadam
    Prasadam, or the sanctified food offering, is a central aspect of the temple's food practices. The prasadam is prepared in the temple kitchen, known as the madapalli, by specially appointed cooks who adhere to strict purity and hygiene standards.

  • Annadanam
    The Ekambaranathar Temple also practices annadanam, which involves the free distribution of meals to devotees, especially during festivals and special occasions. Annadanam is considered one of the highest forms of charity in Hinduism.
Sri Arulmigu Ramanathaswamy Temple
Festivals
Maha Shivaratri
Maha Shivaratri is one of the most important festivals celebrated at the Ramanathaswamy Temple. Dedicated to Lord Shiva, this festival is observed with great devotion, typically in February or March.
Thirukalyanam
Thirukalyanam, the celestial wedding of Lord Shiva and Goddess Parvati, is a major event at the temple. It symbolizes the divine union of Shiva and Shakti, the male and female principles of the universe.

Aadi Amavasya
Aadi Amavasya, observed on the new moon day of the Tamil month of Aadi (July-August), is an important festival in the Ramanathaswamy Temple.
Religious significance

Connection to Lord Rama and the Ramayana: According to the Ramayana, Lord Rama, after defeating the demon king Ravana in Lanka, wanted to absolve himself of the sin of killing Ravana, who was a Brahmin by birth.
One of the Twelve Jyotirlingas: The Ramanathaswamy Temple is one of the twelve Jyotirlingas, which are sacred shrines dedicated to Lord Shiva, where he is believed to have manifested as a column of light.

Architectural Features

Grand Corridors: One of the most striking features of the Ramanathaswamy Temple is its massive corridor. It measures 1,200 meters in length and consists of nearly 1,000 granite pillars. This corridor is known as the "Chokkatan Mandapam".
Gopurams: The temple has two prominent gopurams, one on the eastern side and the other on the western side. The eastern gopuram, which is the main entrance, is about 53 meters (173 feet) high.

Food Practices

Prasadam: Prasad, or sacred food offered to the deity, is prepared daily and is an essential aspect of worship at the temple. The prasad is typically made from ingredients like rice, lentils, jaggery, coconut, and ghee, which are considered pure and auspicious.
Annadhanam: The temple provides meals to devotees as part of its charitable activities, known as Annadhanam. This practice aligns with the temple’s role in promoting community welfare and spirituality.

Sri Ranganatha Swamy Temple
History:
The origins of the Sri Ranganatha Swamy Temple can be traced back to ancient times. The temple is dedicated to Lord Ranganatha, a form of Lord Vishnu reclining on the serpent Adishesha. It is mentioned in several ancient texts, including the Puranas, which describe its divine significance and the legends associated with it.
Architecture:
Seven Prakaras (Enclosures): The temple is surrounded by seven concentric walls, known as prakaras, each enclosing a larger area than the previous one. This layout symbolizes the spiritual journey from the material world to the divine presence. The outermost prakara is known as the Rajagopuram, which serves as the main entrance.
Festivals:
Panguni Uthiram: This festival marks the divine wedding of Lord Ranganatha with his consort, Ranganayaki (also known as Lakshmi).
Vaikunta Ekadasi: Vaikunta Ekadasi is a major festival dedicated to Lord Vishnu and is believed to be the day when the gates of Vaikunta are opened.
Rathasapthami: Rathasapthami, also known as the “Festival of Chariots,” celebrates the annual procession of the deity in a grand chariot. The festival is dedicated to the Sun God and marks the beginning of the solar month.
